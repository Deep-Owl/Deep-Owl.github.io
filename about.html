Artist Bio:

Devin Powell is a multimedia artist and student based out of Columbus Ohio who embraces the challenge of growth and explores diverse mediums such as ceramics, sound, and robotics. He enjoys the problem solving aspect of writing software and interfacing with electronics and is currently focusing on improving his abilities with technology. He has been named on the Dean's list consecutively as an undergraduate at the Ohio State University and was recently hired as an assistant to emeritus faculty and artist Ken Rinaldo following a robotics internship.


Artist Statement:

 When I can’t find the words to describe my thoughts and feelings, and often turn to nonverbal forms of communication such as visualization and sound. The idea of expression is paramount to my work. While sometimes my projects are influenced by interests and aesthetics, I’ve found that my more conceptual work often revolves around themes rooted in the human experience – relationships, healing, empathy, memory, passion, esotericism and community. I'm drawn to the concept of facilitating individuals to encounter themselves and acknowledge the aspects of life that bubble beneath the surface. I have been exploring how to extract meaning from and manifest these concepts through abstraction, by demonstrating collective experiences, and through immersive environments. I also find satisfaction in revealing facets of myself that others can connect with or shedding light on aspects of others that often remain unexplored. It's about introducing us to ourselves, fostering a sense of visibility and presence within our ongoing life narrative.

 Recently I have been thinking about how to express concepts through more technical means. One of my latest works was an exercise realized through microcontroller units and computer language. I created a dynamic, sound-reactive sculpture using microcontrollers, a microphone, 204 LEDs, custom 3D printed gears, lumber and a motor and various electronic components. The finished project is less conceptual than some of my other works but served as a necessary steppingstone on my journey in a new field of practice. As the project neared completion, I imagined the potential of populating a space with many of these interactive sculptures, inviting people to immerse themselves in work, which was originally about how we respond to the physical presence of others.
